PMO Wave 75 - The New GOAT

- Here’s my review of a new, lesser-known keyboard. I hope you’ll enjoy it!

Moderator

Background

First of all, I’m a keyboard enthusiast from Vietnam. Since the beginning of this year, I’ve already tried, tested, bought, and sold over 25 keyboards.
This time, I’m testing the PMO Wave75.


The unboxing experience is quite premium. The keyboard comes wrapped in plastic film with an acrylic dust cover. Accessories include spare switches, a keycap/switch puller, extra foam layers, mounting rubbers, and screws for different mounting styles.

Build Quality and Features

The build quality is excellent.
The Wave75 rivals the Rainy75, previously my favorite. The standard version has HMX switches and a 4,000 mAh battery, while the pro version includes Kaihua switches, an FR4 plate, and an 8,000 mAh battery. The HMX switches feel smoother and sound better to me.

Unfortunately, each board color is tied to a specific version, limiting customization.
For example, the red version is only available as the pro version.

Aesthetic and Design

The red anodized version is stunning with its gold-tone backplate.
The green version has a subtle glitter effect.
Though the backplate design is simple, it includes a dongle storage compartment, which is a big plus.

Typing and Sound

Both versions sound great.
I prefer the standard version’s sound profile. It has VIA support and a solid ball-catch system.
The keycaps are double-shot, and the stabilizers are well-lubed.
The RGB lighting is bright and adjustable. The logo also lights up for indicators like Caps Lock—a thoughtful touch.

Final Thoughts

The Wave75 is easily one of the best keyboards in its price range. It offers premium build quality, VIA support, dongle storage, bright RGB, and a unique RGB indicator logo. My only issue is the inconvenient placement of the on/off switch under the Caps Lock key.

Feel free to ask any questions! I’ll be swapping the switches for Vertexes and the keycaps for Cerakeys on the red pro version soon, so stay tuned for an update!

A budget alu board with VIA support is always welcome as an alternative, but come on - let's not call something the "GOAT" when it still shares all its competitors' flaws: 1.2mm PCB, flex cuts galore, very limited plate material choices, and only tri-mode version available. Also, you completely missed the one thing that might have actually set this apart from the Rainy and Bridge and all the budget 75s out there: Wave75 supports both bog-standard gasket and silicone gasket bean for mounting. I think it's the first in that price range that can be mounted in two ways, although whether or not the two mounting styles offer any noticeable difference is another matter entirely.
